Dalai Lama


Born: 6th July 1935 (age 90)
Taktser, Amdo, Tibet

Alma mater: Monastic education at Drepung and Sera Monasteries
Occupation: Spiritual leader of Tibetan Buddhism
Works: The Art of Happiness, Freedom in Exile, My Land and My People
Awards Nobel Peace Prize (1989), Congressional Gold Medal (2007), Templeton Prize (2012)

Tibet, often known as the Roof of the World, is one of the most unique and awe-inspiring regions on Earth. Resting high on the Himalayan plateau, Tibet is a land of snow-capped mountains, vast grasslands, and crystal-clear lakes. It is home to an ancient civilization rooted in peace, compassion, and harmony with nature. For centuries, Tibet has been a place of spiritual depth and cultural richness, known for its monasteries, prayer flags fluttering in the wind, and the wisdom of its people. In the mid 20th century, Tibet was annexed by China, marking a period of great change in its history. This moment reshaped its politics, culture, and environment. While the topic remains deeply discussed around the world, it also draws attention to Tibet’s continuing importance to humanity. Tibet is not only a symbol of spiritual resilience and cultural identity but also an essential part of Earth’s ecological balance. Scientifically, Tibet is often called the Third Pole. Its glaciers and snowfields store more fresh water than anywhere else except the North and South Poles. The rivers that begin in Tibet flow across Asia and provide life to nearly two billion people in countries such as China, India, Nepal, and Bangladesh. The health of these rivers depends on the stability of Tibet’s environment. Climate change and human activities threaten the delicate ecosystem of the plateau. Melting glaciers, desertification, and biodiversity loss could have far-reaching impacts far beyond Tibet itself. Protecting Tibet’s environment is not only about preserving a beautiful landscape, but also about safeguarding water, climate, and life for a large part of the world. Tibet stands as a reminder that nature and culture are deeply connected. Its mountains and rivers do not belong to one nation alone but to all of humanity. Preserving its beauty, culture, and fragile environment is an act of respect for both the planet and the generations to come.
